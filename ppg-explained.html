What a PPG sensor actually measures

It does not measure heart rate. It does not quite measure blood flow either. It measures pulses of blood volume in your skin, using light. Everything else, heart rate included, is calculated from that one wave.

A source-backed walk-through, plus the line to use next time it comes up.

what it measures

Pulsatile change in blood volume in the microvessels, read as a change in how much light returns to a detector.

"blood flow"

Close, but not the same thing. Flow is rate of movement. PPG sees volume. True flow is a derived index, not the raw signal.

"heart rate"

Not measured. Heart rate is computed from the spacing between volume pulses in the waveform.

01 / The setup

A light, a detector, and your blood in between

A reflectance PPG sensor sits on the skin. An LED shines light in. A photodiode next to it reads how much light bounces back. Blood absorbs more light than the tissue around it, so the answer changes with every heartbeat.

More blood in the vessel, more light absorbed, less light returns. The amber ray dims on every beat.
02 / The signal

The wave is volume, riding on a baseline

The returning light traces a wave. The fast wiggle, the AC component, is arterial blood volume rising and falling with each heartbeat. It sits on a slow DC baseline from static tissue, veins, and bone. Count the peaks and you have heart rate. The signal itself was never heart rate.

PPG waveform · live— bpm
AC · pulsatile blood volume DC · static tissue baseline each peak = one heartbeat

This is why a wrist or temple optical sensor can read heart rate, breathing, and blood oxygen without a single wire touching an artery. One optical wave, many readouts.

A PPG sensor optically measures pulsatile changes in blood volume. Heart rate, HRV, oxygen, and perfusion are all derived from that one waveform. So "it measures heart rate" skips three steps, and "it measures blood flow" names the wrong variable.

04 / The disagreement, graded

Three statements, one correct

The original claim"PPG measures blood flow, and from that you get heart rate."Half right

The direction is correct: the optical signal comes first, heart rate is derived from it. The variable is wrong: PPG reads blood volume, not flow. Flow is how fast blood moves; volume is how much sits in the vessel. PPG sees volume. Real flow shows up only as a derived perfusion index.

The product head's reply"No, the technology measures heart rate."Also imprecise

The sensor does not measure heart rate. It measures the blood volume wave, and heart rate is computed from the gaps between peaks. Calling the output the measurement skips the actual physics. Strictly, this is one step further from the truth than the first claim.

The precise version"PPG optically measures pulsatile blood volume in the skin. Heart rate is derived from the pulse timing."Correct

Raw signal: blood volume, via light absorption. Everything else, heart rate, HRV, SpO2, perfusion, is a calculation layered on top. Say this and you are ahead of the room.

05 / Is it like a CGM patch?

Same idea, different machine

A continuous glucose monitor and an optical wearable feel similar, a patch on the body streaming a live metabolic number. Under the hood they share almost nothing.

CGM patchPPG wearable (Temple-family)
MethodElectrochemical, an enzyme reaction makes a currentOptical, light absorbed by blood
InvasivenessMinimally invasive, a filament sits under the skinNon-invasive, nothing breaks the skin
What it readsGlucose in interstitial fluidBlood volume, plus oxygenation
Delay5 to 10 minutes (interstitial lag)Real-time, beat to beat
the honest framing

Same product spirit, a continuous metabolic signal you wear. Completely different sensing. One pokes into interstitial fluid and runs a chemical reaction. The other reads light bouncing off blood. And note: optical sensors give oxygenation cleanly, but true blood flow usually needs more than plain PPG, which is the right question to put back to Temple's product team.
